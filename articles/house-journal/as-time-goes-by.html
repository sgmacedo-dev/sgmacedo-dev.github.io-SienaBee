As Time Goes By

Reflections on beauty, memory and the quiet grace of things that endure.

Approx. 4-minute read

Music of the House Johnny Mathis
As Time Goes By

At the Writing Desk Darjeeling Tea
Fine Porcelain Cup

This morning, the House awoke to the gentle voice of Johnny Mathis.

Some melodies do not merely accompany a passing hour; they settle quietly into the architecture of memory, until one can scarcely distinguish where the music ends and remembrance begins.

There is a peculiar nobility in those compositions which have survived generations without clamouring for attention. They neither compete nor persuade. They simply endure.

The same, perhaps, may be said of genuine beauty. Not the variety that seeks admiration through novelty, but the quieter form that reveals itself only to those who have cultivated the patience to notice.

An old fountain pen whose brass has grown warm beneath faithful hands. A linen tablecloth carefully mended across the years. A favourite armchair beside a rain-lit window. A handwritten letter whose paper has softened with time. A melody capable of quieting an entire room decades after it was first composed.

Perhaps elegance has never belonged to rarity. Perhaps it has always belonged to permanence.

Modern civilisation often mistakes speed for significance. It celebrates immediacy, confuses visibility with importance, and rewards whatever is newest, loudest, or most easily consumed. Yet almost everything worthy of lifelong affection requires precisely the opposite conditions. Friendship. Character. Scholarship. Craftsmanship. Wisdom. None of these flourish in haste.

There is a remarkable serenity in objects, places, and even people who no longer strive to prove themselves. Like old libraries, they possess an authority that never announces itself.

One suspects that true refinement is not accumulated. It is distilled. Everything unnecessary gradually falls away, until only substance remains.

Perhaps this explains why certain houses, certain books, and certain conversations continue to accompany us long after their outward forms have faded. They do not occupy memory through spectacle. They inhabit it through quiet fidelity.

The House of Siena Bee was never conceived as another hurried destination upon the endless avenues of the internet. Its ambition is considerably more modest— and, for that very reason, perhaps rather more enduring.

May these pages become a place to which thoughtful readers return without urgency. A room where silence is not emptiness, but hospitality. A library where every essay may be read as one might receive an old and trusted friend.

If, on some quiet afternoon years hence, you should discover yourself returning here without quite remembering how you first arrived, then this House shall have fulfilled the purpose for which it was built.

Written for the House Journal

Silvana Macedo Founder & Editor · Siena Bee
